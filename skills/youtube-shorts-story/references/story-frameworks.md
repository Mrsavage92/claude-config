# Story Frameworks for Curiosity Shorts

Every framework below exists to serve one KPI: average view duration (AVD) greater than video length. That only happens when the story earns a full watch AND a rewatch or loop. Pick the framework per video; do not blend three frameworks into one 30-second script.

## Script word budget (hard physics, not style)

TTS narration lands at ~2.5 words/second at natural pace. Zack D. Films-style scripts run 65-95 words for ~30 seconds. Budget:

| Target length | Max words | Shots (at 1.5-3s each) |
|---|---|---|
| 20s | 50 | 8-12 |
| 30s | 75 | 10-16 |
| 40s | 100 | 14-20 |
| 60s | 150 | 20-30 |

If the script exceeds budget, cut explanation, never the payoff. A short that explains 70% of something completely beats one that explains 100% of it rushed.

## The Zack D. spine (default structure)

Reverse-engineered from his output and his own scriptwriter job listing: **shocking visual premise (0-3s) → visual reveal → fast explanation → twist or payoff**. Rules that make it work:

- Open in medias res. The first line IS the premise. Verbal windups ("Did you know...", "Today I'll explain...") are banned; they spend the only 2 seconds that matter on throat-clearing.
- Extreme word economy with sensory verbs ("his heart hammered against his ribs", not "his heart rate increased").
- The visual carries the discomfort or wonder; the narration carries the facts. If narration describes what the viewer can already see, delete the line.
- End on the twist, not on a summary. Never end with "so that's why...".

## Closed loop (streamlined storyline, the default)

One question, opened in the first 2 seconds, answered by the end, with the final beat visually or verbally reconnecting to the opening. This is the workhorse: self-contained, satisfying, and it sets up the rewatch loop (see retention-playbook.md). Test: can you state the video's question in one sentence? If it takes two sentences, split into two videos.

## Open loop (Zeigarnik effect)

An unresolved gap between what the brain knows and what it wants to know creates discomfort that demands closure. Mechanics:

- Open with an incomplete statement or a "but... then" structure: the "but" creates the problem, the "then" opens the question ("Divers found the ship intact, but when they opened the cargo hold...").
- Withhold the resolution until the final 3-5 seconds.
- Mid-video, add ONE secondary mini-loop (a detail promised and paid off 10 seconds later) to bridge the retention dip.

Use open loops WITHIN a video always; use loops ACROSS videos (cliffhanger endings pointing to a part 2) only after the channel has data showing part-2s get comparable views, because an unresolved ending on a weak video reads as a broken promise.

## Start-at-the-end (result-first)

Show the climax or aftermath first, then rewind to how it happened. This is the strongest structure when the payoff visual is more striking than the setup ("This entire bridge is about to disappear. Here's why."). It also doubles as the "anti-hook" (below).

## Anti-hook (result-first pattern interrupt)

"Anti-hook" is informal creator shorthand, not an established term; the documented equivalent is result-first / pattern interrupt. The idea: skip hype phrasing entirely and drop straight into outcome or action. No promise, no "you won't believe", just the most interesting frame and sentence of the video, immediately. When the feed is full of hyped hooks, the calm confident cold-open IS the pattern interrupt. Use it when the subject is inherently strong; use a question hook when the subject needs framing to become interesting.

## A/B storytelling (two meanings, use both deliberately)

1. **Dual-thread narrative (A-plot / B-plot):** interleave two related threads so closing one opens the other (A: what the astronaut felt; B: what mission control saw). Only for 45-60s videos; a 30s video has no room for two threads.
2. **A/B hook testing:** YouTube's native A/B test tool does NOT apply to Shorts. The working method: produce two versions differing ONLY in the first 2-3 seconds, post them spaced apart (or one unlisted trial), then compare retention-graph SHAPE in YouTube Studio (Engagement -> Audience retention). A winning hook shows a flat curve through the first third before gradual decay. Testable variables: hook wording, first-frame composition, title. Not testable: mid-video pacing (too many confounds).

Every VIDEO-PLAN includes 3 hook variants for this reason, even when only one gets produced first.

## Question-led childlike-curiosity hooks

The curiosity taxonomy that fits a children-to-adult general audience. Documented hook patterns that map to this channel (from the 14-pattern taxonomy at shorta.ai plus curiosity-gap research):

- **Question hook:** "Why do your ears pop on planes?" Direct, honest, answerable in 30s.
- **What-happens-if:** "What happens if you never cut your fingernails?" The viewer's own body/world made strange.
- **Hidden mechanism:** "This is what's actually inside a golf ball." The cutaway/X-ray instinct.
- **Expert secret:** "Pilots know something about turbulence that passengers don't."
- **Scale shift:** "If the Sun were the size of a basketball, Earth would be a peppercorn 26 meters away."
- **Forbidden/impossible:** "Why you can't dig a hole to China" (safe transgression, not gore or sexuality).

Pick topics where the QUESTION is instantly understood by a 10-year-old but the ANSWER surprises an adult. That overlap is the channel's whole positioning.

## Hook quality bar

A hook passes only if all four are true:
1. Complete within 2.5 seconds (the drop-off cliff is at 3s).
2. Phrased in words a 10-year-old knows.
3. The first FRAME alone (no audio) makes someone stop scrolling; it doubles as the de facto thumbnail.
4. The video actually answers it. Curiosity gap, never clickbait; a broken promise trains the audience to skip the channel.
